How a Newly Placed Order is Managed in Magento 2 Store: A Guide

The Default Processing Path of an Order

The sequence of steps involved in processing may vary depending on a store’s business practices. Here is a typical processing flow:

  • Placement: The customer completes their shopping and clicks the checkout button on the checkout page, triggering the start of processing.
  • Registration: The system creates a new one with a “pending” status, waiting for payment confirmation. The store manager can edit or cancel the before payment.
  • Payment received: Upon payment confirmation by the store manager, the status changes to “Processing”. The customer may receive a notification from their chosen payment provider about the transaction status.
  • Invoice creation: The store creates an invoice for the customer after receiving payment, with options varying based on the payment method used. A copy of the invoice is sent to the customer.
  • Shipment: The store manager arranges for shipment, and creates a packing slip, and shipping label. The customer is notified of the shipment and can track the package using a tracking service in their account.

Limitations of Magento in Order Management

A common scenario for a store is the need to modify order details, whether due to incomplete information from the customer during checkout, changes in information during processing, or the customer’s request to add or change products. Understanding how a newly placed order is managed in the Magento 2 store is important to ensure that these modifications can be made efficiently and accurately, without compromising the customer experience.

However, Magento 2 has a limitation where data cannot be altered once it is processed in the backend and stored in the database.

To overcome the limitations of managing in the backend, stores can use the Magento Management extension. This tool enhances the backend management system’s flexibility in handling. Store managers can easily modify existing orders without having to cancel them first. They can manage current requests, add items, impose restrictions, and more, saving time and reducing errors.
